📍 Quick Answer — Where Should You Go in Costa Rica?

The best places to visit in Costa Rica are Arenal Volcano (La Fortuna), Manuel Antonio National Park, the Monteverde Cloud Forest, the Guanacaste beaches around Tamarindo, and Tortuguero National Park. For raw wilderness, add Corcovado on the Osa Peninsula. A classic first-timer route links Arenal, Monteverde and a Pacific beach over 8–10 days. Costa Rica packs volcanoes, cloud forests, two coastlines and roughly 5% of the planet's biodiversity into an area smaller than West Virginia — which is exactly why it rewards a focused, well-planned itinerary over trying to see everything at once.

🌴 The Pura Vida Country

Why Costa Rica Belongs at the Top of Your List

There is no other country on Earth quite like Costa Rica. In a single morning you can stand at the rim of an active volcano, and by sunset be watching surfers carve glassy Pacific waves. Drive three hours and the landscape shifts from dry tropical forest to dripping cloud forest to steamy lowland jungle. Costa Rica is roughly the size of a small U.S. state, yet it holds an estimated five percent of all the biodiversity on the planet — a statistic that sounds like marketing until you spend a day in one of its national parks and lose count of the species you've seen.

What sets Costa Rica apart is not just nature, but how seriously the country protects it. Nearly twenty-eight percent of the national territory sits inside protected national parks and reserves. The country abolished its army in 1948 and redirected that spending toward education, health and conservation. The result is a place where ecotourism isn't a buzzword bolted onto a beach resort — it's the backbone of the entire economy, and it shows in the quality of the guides, the trails, and the wildlife encounters waiting around nearly every corner.

And then there's pura vida. Literally "pure life," it's the national philosophy you'll hear a dozen times a day — a greeting, a goodbye, a shrug at bad luck, a celebration of good. It captures the unhurried, grateful, deeply content rhythm of Costa Rican life. Visitors arrive chasing zip lines and end up staying for the feeling. The places below are the ones that earn their spot on a Costa Rica itinerary — organized so you can build a trip that actually flows instead of zig-zagging across the country.

⭐ The Essential List

The 9 Best Places to Visit in Costa Rica

Ranked and explained — from the can't-miss icons every first-timer should see to the wild, hard-to-reach corners that reward the adventurous. Each one earns its place for a different reason.

1
Volcano · Adventure

Arenal Volcano & La Fortuna

Northern Lowlands

The near-perfect cone of Arenal is Costa Rica's most iconic image. The town of La Fortuna at its base is the country's adventure capital — natural hot springs fed by the volcano, the thundering La Fortuna Waterfall, hanging bridges through the canopy, zip lines, and white-water rafting all within minutes.

Hot SpringsWaterfallsZip Lining
2
Beach · Wildlife

Manuel Antonio National Park

Central Pacific Coast

The country's most beloved national park is the rare place where rainforest spills directly onto white-sand beaches. Squirrel monkeys, three-toed sloths, iguanas and capuchins live right beside the trails. Small, accessible and endlessly photogenic — it's the easiest big wildlife hit in Costa Rica.

BeachesMonkeysSloths
3
Cloud Forest · Nature

Monteverde Cloud Forest

Central Highlands

High in the mountains, Monteverde is a mystical world of mist-shrouded trees dripping with moss and orchids. Walk hanging bridges suspended in the canopy, hunt for the legendary resplendent quetzal, and feel the temperature drop as clouds drift through the forest around you. Utterly unlike anywhere else on the trip.

Hanging BridgesBirdingHiking
4
Beach · Surf

Tamarindo & Guanacaste Beaches

North Pacific (Guanacaste)

Guanacaste is Costa Rica's sun-soaked beach belt, anchored by lively Tamarindo. Reliable surf for every level, sunset bars, sportfishing, and easy day trips to quieter gems like Playa Conchal, Nosara and Santa Teresa. Liberia airport (LIR) makes this the fastest beach-first landing in the country.

SurfingSunsetsNightlife
5
Jungle · Turtles

Tortuguero National Park

Caribbean Coast

Often called Costa Rica's Amazon, Tortuguero is a roadless maze of jungle canals reachable only by boat or small plane. Glide past caimans, river otters and toucans, then watch sea turtles nest on the black-sand beaches (July–October). A true off-grid wildlife immersion.

Canal ToursSea TurtlesBoat Only
6
Wilderness · Remote

Corcovado & the Osa Peninsula

South Pacific

National Geographic once called the Osa Peninsula "the most biologically intense place on Earth." Corcovado is the last great Pacific lowland rainforest — home to all four Costa Rican monkey species, tapirs, scarlet macaws and big cats. Remote, raw and guide-required, it's the ultimate reward for serious nature lovers.

RainforestRare WildlifeOff-Grid
7
Beach · Wellness

Nicoya Peninsula (Blue Zone)

Northwest Pacific

One of the world's five Blue Zones, where people routinely live past 100. The Nicoya Peninsula blends that longevity culture with yoga-and-surf towns like Santa Teresa and Mal País, empty beaches, and a slower, soulful pace. The wellness traveler's Costa Rica.

YogaLongevitySurf
8
Caribbean · Culture

Puerto Viejo & the Caribbean Coast

South Caribbean

The Caribbean side marches to a different drum — Afro-Caribbean culture, reggae rhythms, spicy rondón stew and coconut-rich cooking. Puerto Viejo and Cahuita offer laid-back beaches, the Jaguar Rescue Center, snorkeling reefs, and a bohemian energy you won't find on the Pacific.

ReefsCultureCuisine
9
City · Culture

San José & the Central Valley

Central Valley

Most trips begin in the capital, and it's worth a day. San José holds the Gold and Jade museums, the ornate National Theatre, vibrant Central Market food stalls, and the surrounding Central Valley coffee farms. A useful base for the Poás and Irazú volcanoes and a real taste of everyday Tico life.

MuseumsCoffeeGateway

🗺️ Know The Regions

Costa Rica, Region by Region

Understanding the seven regions is the single best way to plan a Costa Rica trip that flows. Tap any region to see what it's known for and which destinations belong to it.

    🧳 Planning Note

    How to Tie These Places Together

    The most common first-timer mistake is trying to see all of Costa Rica in one trip. Distances look small on a map, but mountain roads, river crossings and single-lane bridges mean a 90-mile drive can eat half a day. The smarter move is to pick two or three regions that pair well and go deep. The undisputed champion route links La Fortuna (Arenal), Monteverde and a Pacific beach — three completely different ecosystems within a comfortable triangle, doable in 8 to 10 days with time to actually relax.

    If you have two weeks, you can stretch that core loop in one of two directions. Wildlife-focused travelers should bolt on Tortuguero or the Osa Peninsula, trading a little comfort for the kind of biodiversity that turns a nice vacation into the trip of a lifetime. Culture-and-beach travelers might instead add the Caribbean coast — Puerto Viejo and Cahuita — for a complete change of food, music and pace. And if you're flying into Liberia for a beach-first trip, you can flip the whole sequence: start with Guanacaste sunshine, work inland to Monteverde and Arenal, and finish at the airport you started from. There's no single correct order. The goal is simply to minimize backtracking and give each place enough time to actually sink in.

    🐾 What You'll See

    The Wildlife That Makes Costa Rica Famous

    You don't have to be a hardcore naturalist to be floored by Costa Rican wildlife — it's everywhere, often right beside the trail or the pool. Here's the cast of characters you'll be hoping to spot.

    🦥

    Sloths

    Both two- and three-toed sloths hang in the canopy. Best spotted with a guide's scope near Manuel Antonio and the Caribbean.

    🐒

    Four Monkey Species

    Howler, spider, capuchin and tiny squirrel monkeys. You'll hear howlers at dawn long before you see them.

    🦜

    Scarlet Macaws

    Brilliant red, blue and gold parrots, best seen on the Osa Peninsula and Carara. They fly in lifelong pairs.

    🐸

    Poison Dart Frogs

    Tiny, jewel-bright and unmistakable. The red-and-blue 'blue jeans' frog is a Caribbean lowland favorite.

    🐢

    Sea Turtles

    Nesting greens at Tortuguero and arribadas of olive ridleys at Ostional — one of nature's great spectacles.

    🦅

    Resplendent Quetzal

    The holy grail of Central American birding, with shimmering green tail feathers. Monteverde and San Gerardo de Dota are top spots.

    📅 Timing Your Trip

    The Best Time to Visit Costa Rica

    Costa Rica has two seasons, not four, and both have real advantages. Your ideal window depends on whether you're chasing sunshine, lower prices, or fewer crowds.

    ☀️

    December – April (Dry Season)

    Sunshine, blue skies and the easiest travel conditions. This is peak season — book hotels and tours well ahead, especially over Christmas, New Year and Easter week (Semana Santa). Prices are highest, but the Pacific beaches are at their best.

    Best: First-timers & beaches
    🌿

    May – November (Green Season)

    Often unfairly called the 'rainy season.' Mornings are usually bright and sunny; rain tends to come in the afternoon. The landscape is impossibly lush, waterfalls roar, prices drop, and crowds thin. Smart travelers love it — just pack a rain jacket.

    Best: Value & lush scenery
    🐢

    Wildlife & Special Windows

    Turtle nesting at Tortuguero peaks July–October. Whale watching off the South Pacific runs roughly July–November and again December–March. Quetzal sightings are best in the breeding months of March–June. Plan around the wildlife you most want to see.

    Best: Nature timing

      💡 Before You Go

      Practical Tips for Visiting Costa Rica

      Getting there and around. Two international airports serve the country: Juan Santamaría (SJO) near San José in the Central Valley, and Daniel Oduber Quirós (LIR) in Liberia, far closer to the Guanacaste beaches. If your trip is beach-heavy, flying into Liberia can save you four-plus hours of driving. Renting a 4x4 gives you the most freedom, but shared shuttles and domestic flights on small airlines are excellent for longer hops like San José to Tortuguero or the Osa Peninsula.

      Money, safety and language. The currency is the Costa Rican colón, but U.S. dollars are widely accepted and cards work almost everywhere. Costa Rica is among the safest countries in Central America, though normal precautions apply — don't leave valuables in parked rental cars, a perennial tip. Spanish is the official language; English is common in tourist areas, but a few friendly words of Spanish go a long way with Ticos, who are famously warm and helpful.

      Travel responsibly. Costa Rica's magic exists because it's protected. Choose certified sustainable tour operators, never touch or feed wildlife, stay on marked trails, and respect park rules. The country has clawed back its forests from heavy deforestation precisely because visitors and locals value them — being a thoughtful guest keeps it that way for the next traveler. That's pura vida in action: leave a place as beautiful as you found it.

      🍛 Taste & Culture

      Food, Coffee & the Tico Way of Life

      Costa Rican food is honest, fresh and built around what the land gives generously: rice, black beans, plantains, tropical fruit and just-caught fish. The dish you'll meet first is the casado — a hearty lunch plate of rice, beans, sweet fried plantain, a simple salad and your choice of chicken, beef, pork or fish. Breakfast means gallo pinto, the beloved rice-and-beans mix sizzled with onion, peppers and a dash of Salsa Lizano, the slightly sweet national condiment found on every table. It's comfort food in the truest sense, and a single casado at a roadside soda (a small family-run eatery) often costs a fraction of a tourist-restaurant meal while tasting twice as good.

      Then there's the coffee, and in Costa Rica that's no afterthought. The volcanic soil and high-altitude slopes of the Central Valley, Tarrazú and the surrounding highlands produce some of the most respected beans in the world. A coffee-farm tour — walking the rows, watching the cherries get sorted, washed and roasted, then cupping the result — is one of the most rewarding half-days you can spend, and it connects you to the agricultural heart of the country. Pair it with a chunk of fresh tropical fruit you've never heard of (mamón chino, guanábana, granadilla) and you start to understand why Ticos seem so unbothered by the rush of the modern world.

      Culturally, Costa Rica rewards travelers who slow down. The famously friendly Ticos value warmth and politeness over speed; a greeting and a smile open doors that brusqueness never will. Family, community and the outdoors sit at the center of life. You'll see it in the soccer games that spill onto every patch of flat grass, the Sunday gatherings that stretch all afternoon, and the easy generosity of strangers who'll walk you to the bus stop rather than just point. Tipping is appreciated but not aggressive; a service charge is usually already built into restaurant bills. Lean into the rhythm — it's the whole point of coming.

      ✨ Bucket-List Moments

      The Experiences You'll Be Telling Stories About

      Beyond the destinations themselves, certain Costa Rica experiences have a way of lodging permanently in memory. Soaking in a natural hot spring at the foot of Arenal as warm rain falls and the jungle hums around you. Stepping onto a hanging bridge in Monteverde and realizing you're eye-level with the canopy, a layer of the forest most people never see. Hearing the deep, prehistoric roar of howler monkeys echo across the treetops at first light — a sound that feels far too large for the animals making it. These moments cost little and ask only that you show up and pay attention.

      If you want the adrenaline version, Costa Rica delivers in abundance. Zip-lining was effectively pioneered here, and the country still has some of the longest and fastest canopy cables on the planet, many of them strung across cloud-forest valleys in Monteverde. White-water rafting on the Pacuare River — repeatedly ranked among the world's most scenic rafting runs — sends you through gorges walled with waterfalls and untouched rainforest. Surfers chase the consistent swells of Tamarindo, Santa Teresa and Pavones, the latter home to one of the longest left-hand waves in the world. And for a gentler thrill, a sunset horseback ride along an empty Pacific beach is hard to beat.

      What ties it all together is scale and contrast. Nowhere else can you raft a wild river in the morning, tour a coffee farm in the afternoon, and soak in a volcanic spring under the stars that same night — all without a long flight in between. That density of experience is Costa Rica's real superpower, and it's why so many travelers who plan a one-time trip end up coming back again and again. Build your itinerary around the moments that move you most, give yourself permission to do less and savor more, and the country will reward you with the kind of trip you measure your other vacations against.

      💰 What It Costs

      How Much Does a Costa Rica Trip Really Cost?

      Costa Rica has a reputation as the priciest country in Central America, and there's truth to it — but the range is enormous, and you have far more control over the number than most people assume. At the budget end, backpackers and surfers get by comfortably on 50 to 70 U.S. dollars a day by staying in hostels and small cabinas, eating at sodas, taking public buses, and choosing free or low-cost nature over packaged tours. The public bus network is genuinely excellent and absurdly cheap, connecting nearly every town for a few dollars; it's slow, but it's an adventure in itself and a window into everyday Tico life.

      The mid-range sweet spot, where most travelers land, runs roughly 120 to 250 dollars per person per day. That covers comfortable boutique hotels and eco-lodges, a rental car for freedom, a couple of guided tours, and the freedom to eat wherever looks good. At the luxury end, Costa Rica competes with anywhere — all-inclusive Guanacaste resorts, private rainforest villas with butler service, and remote eco-lodges that fly you in by small plane can run 500 dollars a day and well beyond. The biggest single variable is usually how you get around: a 4x4 rental plus fuel adds up, but it unlocks the self-drive freedom that makes the classic Arenal–Monteverde–beach loop so rewarding.

      A few honest money tips that save real cash. Travel in the green season (May–November) and the same hotel often costs a third less than in January. Eat at least one meal a day at a soda, where a filling casado costs a fraction of a tourist-restaurant plate. Book tours directly with local operators rather than through hotel concierges who add a markup. And don't over-schedule paid activities — many of Costa Rica's best moments, from a dawn beach walk to spotting a sloth from a public trail, cost nothing at all. The country rewards travelers who slow down, and slowing down also happens to be the cheapest way to do it.
